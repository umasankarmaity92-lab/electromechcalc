Engineers and technicians constantly move between unit systems — metric drawings against imperial nameplates, SI pressure ratings against psi gauges, or a vendor datasheet in BTU when your own calculations run in kilojoules. Detailed Guide

Understanding Each Converter

📏 Length Converter — mm, cm, m, km, inch, ft, yd, mile

Base unit: Meter (m)

Length is the most frequently converted quantity on any engineering drawing or site survey, and it is also the one where small mistakes cause the most expensive rework. A civil or electrical drawing produced to Indian Standard practice will almost always be dimensioned in millimeters or meters, while imported machinery, fasteners, pipe fittings, and older mechanical drawings frequently carry inch-based callouts. The Length Converter here handles both systems side by side — millimeter, centimeter, meter, and kilometer on the metric side, and inch, foot, yard, and mile on the imperial side — using the meter as the internal base unit so every conversion is a single multiplication or division against a fixed factor.

These factors are not approximations. An inch is defined internationally as exactly 25.4 mm, a foot as exactly 0.3048 m, a yard as exactly 0.9144 m, and a statute mile as exactly 1609.344 m. Because these are exact definitions rather than rounded constants, chaining conversions (for example, converting a length from inches to feet to meters and back) will not introduce drift, which matters when a dimension is being carried through several stages of a design calculation.

On site, length conversion shows up constantly: checking a cable tray or conduit run length against a BOQ that mixes meters and feet, confirming that an imported bearing's shaft length in inches matches a metric housing bore, converting a plot or right-of-way distance from miles to kilometers for a transmission line route survey, or verifying that a machined component's tolerance band (often specified in thousandths of an inch, i.e. mils, on imported equipment) is being read correctly against a metric micrometer. Cable and conduit takeoff sheets in India are usually metric, but vendor catalogs for imported valves, actuators, and instrumentation often list dimensions in inches, so a quick, reliable converter avoids errors when cross-referencing a datasheet against a site measurement.

A common mistake when converting length by hand is confusing the multiplication direction — multiplying by the factor when you should be dividing, which silently produces a result that is off by the square or even just inverted. Because this tool always shows the explicit "1 unit = X unit" conversion factor alongside the result, and includes a one-click swap button, it removes that ambiguity. The quick-reference table below the input also converts your entered value into every other length unit simultaneously, which is useful when you need to communicate the same dimension to two teams working in different unit systems — for instance, giving a fabricator a dimension in millimeters while the same value needs to appear in feet on an as-built imperial reference drawing.

It is worth remembering that length conversion is linear and additive-free (unlike temperature), so results scale perfectly — doubling the input value doubles every converted output. This makes it safe to use the quick-reference table as a lookup card: once you know 1 meter equals roughly 3.281 feet, you can mentally scale that ratio for quick estimates on site, then come back to this tool whenever you need the precise, rounding-free value for documentation, tender submissions, or as-built records.

▦ Area Converter — mm², cm², m², km², in², ft², acre, hectare

Base unit: Square Meter (m²)

Area conversion trips people up more often than length conversion because the scaling factor is squared, not linear — a mistake of using the length factor directly on an area value produces an answer that is wrong by a large margin rather than just slightly off. This converter avoids that entirely by storing pre-computed area factors (square millimeter, square centimeter, square meter, square kilometer, square inch, square foot, acre, and hectare) relative to the square meter, so you never need to manually square a length factor yourself.

In Indian engineering and construction contexts, area conversion is central to land and plot documentation. Plot sizes are frequently quoted in units that vary by region and by whether the transaction is formal or informal — square feet is standard in urban real estate and building plan sanctioning, while acres and hectares dominate agricultural land records, industrial land allotments, and solar or wind farm site planning. A site engineer scoping a rooftop solar installation, for example, needs to move fluidly between the roof area in square meters (used in the panel-count and yield calculation) and the plot area in acres or hectares quoted in the land documents, while an electrical engineer sizing a substation yard may need the fenced area in square meters cross-checked against a hectare figure from the land allotment letter.

The acre and hectare are both non-SI units retained because of how deeply embedded they are in legal and administrative land records — 1 acre equals 4,046.8564224 m² and 1 hectare equals exactly 10,000 m². Because these values are large relative to square meters, small rounding errors in manual calculation can translate into a meaningful area discrepancy when scaled up over a multi-acre site, which is why this tool carries full floating-point precision internally and only rounds for on-screen display.

Beyond land, area conversion matters for HVAC duct or panel sheet metal takeoffs (square feet to square meters when comparing a US-origin datasheet against a metric fabrication drawing), cable tray fill calculations that reference cross-sectional area in mm² against conductor area also in mm² per IS 694 / IS 7098 tables, and heat-transfer or insulation area calculations where a surface area in square feet from an imported equipment manual needs to be reconciled with a metric process design package.

As with length, the quick-reference table shows your entered value converted into all eight area units at once, which is a fast way to sanity-check whether a plot size "sounds right" — for instance, immediately seeing that a given hectare figure corresponds to roughly how many square feet helps catch a data-entry error (a hectare mistakenly entered as an acre, for example) before it propagates into a cost estimate or a panel layout calculation.

Solar and renewable energy planning is one of the fastest-growing users of area conversion in India today. A typical rooftop or ground-mount solar sizing exercise starts with an available area in square meters or square feet, needs to be checked against a land parcel documented in acres for a ground-mount project, and finally has to be reconciled with panel datasheet dimensions that may themselves be quoted in millimeters, requiring a length-to-area mental step. Getting this chain of conversions right the first time avoids re-doing a panel layout after discovering the usable area was miscalculated by treating a linear scaling factor as if it applied directly to area.

⚖ Weight Converter — mg, g, kg, metric ton, lb, oz

Base unit: Kilogram (kg)

Weight (mass) conversion sits at the intersection of procurement, logistics, and mechanical design. Indian industry works almost exclusively in the metric scale — milligrams for lab and chemical dosing, grams and kilograms for component and material weights, and the metric ton (1,000 kg) for bulk material, structural steel tonnage, and freight billing. Imported equipment, however, frequently ships with a nameplate or datasheet quoting weight in pounds or ounces, and international freight documentation sometimes mixes both systems on the same shipping manifest.

This converter uses the internationally defined pound as exactly 0.45359237 kg and the ounce as exactly 1/16 of that, so conversions between the metric and imperial weight scales are exact rather than approximated with a rounded 2.2 lb/kg rule of thumb — a shortcut that is fine for a rough mental estimate but introduces compounding error once you are calculating shipping costs, crane lifting capacity margins, or a bill of materials with hundreds of line items.

Typical engineering use cases include converting a transformer or switchgear nameplate weight from pounds to kilograms to confirm it is within a crane or forklift's rated capacity, reconciling a structural steel BOQ quoted in metric tons against a fabricator's imperial-unit mill certificate, converting small hardware or fastener weights between grams and ounces for cost comparison against an overseas supplier's per-ounce pricing, and checking battery or panel weights in kilograms against a rooftop structural load capacity that may be documented in pounds per square foot on an older structural drawing.

A frequent point of confusion is the difference between the short ton (2,000 lb, used in the US) and the metric ton (1,000 kg, used in India and most of the world) — this tool deliberately implements only the metric ton to avoid ambiguity, since the "ton" label alone is one of the most error-prone units in cross-border procurement documents. When working with a US-origin document that uses "ton," it is worth confirming with the supplier whether they mean the short ton or the metric tonne before relying on a converted figure for a structural or lifting calculation.

Because mass conversion is purely multiplicative with no offset, the quick-reference table lets you see a single entered weight expressed simultaneously across all six units — useful when a purchase order needs to be issued in kilograms but the technical justification memo needs the same figure quoted in pounds to match an OEM datasheet reference, avoiding the need to run the same number through a calculator twice.

Weight also feeds directly into transportation and installation planning: a switchgear panel's shipping weight determines the crane or hydra capacity needed on site, a transformer's total weight (oil plus core plus tank) determines the foundation and plinth design, and cumulative material weights across a bill of materials determine freight cost. Because these downstream decisions carry real cost and safety implications, converting weight figures accurately — and cross-checking an imperial nameplate value against the metric figure used in a lifting plan — is a small step that prevents both under-specified lifting equipment and unnecessarily oversized (and expensive) crane hire.

🧭 Pressure Converter — Pa, kPa, bar, psi, atm, mmHg, kgf/cm²

Base unit: Pascal (Pa)

Pressure is arguably the unit most likely to appear in three or four different systems on a single Indian industrial site, all at once. Process instrumentation is often calibrated in kg/cm² (a legacy unit still printed on many pressure gauges across boiler houses, compressors, and hydraulic systems), imported valves and pumps are frequently rated in psi, international engineering standards and most modern instrumentation use bar or kPa, medical and some HVAC vacuum applications reference mmHg, and atmospheric or barometric readings are sometimes expressed in atm.

This converter carries all seven of those units against a Pascal base, using the internationally accepted conversion constants: 1 bar equals exactly 100,000 Pa, 1 psi equals 6,894.757293168 Pa, 1 standard atmosphere equals 101,325 Pa, 1 mmHg equals 133.322387415 Pa, and the legacy kgf/cm² equals 98,066.5 Pa. That last unit — kgf/cm², sometimes just labeled "kg" on an old gauge face — is one of the more common sources of confusion on Indian shop floors, since it is neither purely metric-SI nor imperial, but a gravitational metric unit still in wide field use.

Common applications include converting a compressor discharge pressure from an imported psi-rated gauge into bar to match a locally sourced relief valve's bar rating, checking a boiler operating pressure quoted in kg/cm² against a design pressure specified in kPa on the pressure vessel's IBR (Indian Boiler Regulations) documentation, converting a pump's suction lift specification from mmHg (common in vacuum-related datasheets) into a more familiar bar or kPa figure, and verifying that an imported hydraulic cylinder's psi rating provides adequate margin over a system operating pressure documented in bar.

Because pressure differences of even a few percent can matter for safety margins — relief valve set points, pressure vessel design pressure, hydraulic system ratings — this tool computes to full floating-point precision internally and only rounds the displayed result, and always shows the explicit conversion factor used so the calculation can be independently verified or referenced in a technical note. As with the other categories, this tool provides quick reference conversions for general engineering use; for pressure-critical safety documentation (relief valve sizing, pressure vessel certification, IBR filings), always cross-check the final value against the applicable code or standard rather than relying solely on a web calculator.

Another everyday scenario is troubleshooting a compressed-air or pneumatic system, where line pressure is usually set in bar locally but the pneumatic cylinder or actuator's force rating from an overseas catalog is specified against a psi figure. Getting the pressure units aligned correctly before calculating actuator force (which depends on pressure multiplied by piston area) prevents an actuator being selected with insufficient force margin, which is a subtle but common source of intermittent pneumatic system failures on packaging and process automation lines. Atmospheric pressure conversion also comes up in altitude-dependent equipment derating, where a compressor or diesel generator's output correction factor depends on local barometric pressure relative to standard atmosphere, so converting a site's recorded barometric reading into the same atm or kPa basis used in the manufacturer's derating chart is a necessary first step before applying any altitude correction.

🌡 Temperature Converter — Celsius, Fahrenheit, Kelvin

Offset-based formula, not a simple factor

Temperature is the one converter on this page that cannot be reduced to "multiply by a factor," because Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin do not share a common zero point. Zero on the Celsius scale is the freezing point of water, zero on the Fahrenheit scale is an arbitrary historical reference point roughly 17.8 degrees Celsius below freezing, and zero on the Kelvin scale is absolute zero, about 273.15 degrees below the Celsius freezing point. Because of this, converting temperature requires an offset-and-scale formula rather than a pure ratio: Fahrenheit equals Celsius multiplied by 9/5 plus 32, and Kelvin equals Celsius plus 273.15.

This distinction matters practically, not just mathematically. If you tried to convert a 10-degree Celsius temperature rise into Fahrenheit using the same multiplicative logic as a length or weight conversion, you would get a materially wrong answer unless you correctly separate a temperature value (an absolute reading, which needs the full offset formula) from a temperature difference or delta (a change, which only needs the 9/5 scaling factor with no offset added). This converter is built to handle absolute temperature readings correctly using the full formula in both directions, converting through Celsius internally as the common reference point regardless of which two units you select.

In Indian engineering practice, Celsius is the default for almost everything — ambient design temperature, motor winding temperature rise, transformer oil temperature, HVAC setpoints, and process temperatures. Fahrenheit appears mainly on imported American-origin equipment nameplates, some older US-standard motor and compressor datasheets, and certain instrumentation calibrated for a US client. Kelvin shows up specifically in scientific, thermodynamic, and some electronics contexts — LED color temperature ratings, blackbody radiation calculations, and absolute thermodynamic formulas in heat transfer or refrigeration cycle analysis all use Kelvin because the scale has no negative values and starts at true absolute zero.

A frequent real-world use case is checking a motor's maximum permissible winding temperature, often specified in a US datasheet as a Fahrenheit limit (for example, insulation class ratings referenced against a Fahrenheit ambient plus rise figure), against the Celsius-based insulation class temperature limits used in Indian motor standards (IS 12615 and IEC 60034). Another is converting an imported chiller or HVAC unit's Fahrenheit setpoint range into Celsius for a local building management system, or converting a laboratory or calibration certificate's Kelvin-referenced value back into a familiar Celsius reading for a field technician.

Temperature conversion errors are also a common cause of confusion when reading datasheets for cable ampacity derating, where a permissible conductor temperature and an ambient design temperature are both used in a derating formula — mixing a Fahrenheit value from an imported cable manufacturer's chart into a Celsius-based derating calculation without converting first produces a badly wrong ampacity figure. Because this converter always applies the correct offset formula rather than treating temperature as a simple ratio, it removes that specific class of error from any calculation chain that pulls a reference value from a non-Celsius datasheet.

⚡ Energy Converter — J, kJ, cal, kcal, Wh, kWh, BTU

Base unit: Joule (J)

Energy conversion bridges three different worlds that engineers routinely have to reconcile: the SI world of joules and kilojoules used in thermodynamic and mechanical calculations, the electrical billing world of watt-hours and kilowatt-hours used on every Indian electricity bill and in every solar, battery, or load calculation, and the HVAC and thermal world of BTU (British Thermal Units), which remains the standard rating unit for imported air conditioning and refrigeration equipment despite India otherwise using metric units almost everywhere else.

The conversion factors used here are the standard defined constants: 1 calorie equals 4.184 joules, 1 kilocalorie equals 4,184 joules, 1 watt-hour equals exactly 3,600 joules (since a watt is one joule per second, and there are 3,600 seconds in an hour), 1 kilowatt-hour therefore equals exactly 3,600,000 joules, and 1 BTU equals 1,055.05585262 joules. Because kWh is directly derived from the watt-hour by a factor of 1,000, and because electricity billing, solar panel output, battery sizing, UPS backup time, and inverter load calculations are all denominated in kWh in India, this is one of the most frequently used units on the entire ElectroMechCalc site, even outside this converter.

A very common real-world task is converting an air conditioner's BTU/hr cooling capacity rating (the standard on most nameplates, including many sold in India) into an equivalent kWh-per-hour electrical energy comparison, which is a necessary first step before it can be meaningfully compared to a kW-rated system for a load or backup-power sizing exercise — note that a straight BTU-to-kWh conversion gives cooling energy, not input electrical power, since that additionally depends on the unit's EER or SEER efficiency rating. Another common use is converting a food or nutrition-adjacent kilocalorie figure into joules for a basic thermodynamic calculation, or converting a battery's energy capacity between Wh and kJ when comparing a datasheet quoted in one unit against a design calculation done in the other.

Because this converter treats every unit as a pure multiplicative factor against the joule with no offset (unlike temperature), the quick-reference table is a fast way to see a single energy value expressed across all seven units simultaneously — useful, for example, when a solar inverter's daily energy yield in kWh needs to be quoted in the more globally recognized joule or BTU units for an international equipment comparison sheet.

Battery and UPS backup sizing is another area where energy conversion is used constantly: a battery bank's usable capacity is generally expressed in kWh, but individual cell or battery datasheets often quote capacity in ampere-hours (Ah) at a rated voltage, which first needs to be converted to Wh or kWh (by multiplying Ah by voltage) before it can be compared on equal footing with a load's energy demand also expressed in kWh. Once both figures are in the same energy unit, sizing a backup duration or comparing two different battery chemistries on a like-for-like basis becomes a straightforward division rather than a guessing exercise.

🌊 Flow Rate Converter — m³/s, m³/h, L/s, L/min, US GPM, CFM

Base unit: m³/s (volumetric flow)

Volumetric flow rate conversion is central to pump selection, HVAC ducting, and water or process piping design, and it is another category where Indian metric practice regularly collides with imported equipment rated in US units. Pump datasheets produced locally are almost always in m³/h or L/min, while imported pumps — particularly from US manufacturers — are frequently rated in US gallons per minute (GPM). HVAC airflow, similarly, is specified in cubic feet per minute (CFM) on most air handling unit and fan datasheets regardless of country of origin, because CFM remains the de facto global standard for air-side HVAC ratings even in metric countries.

This tool converts between m³/s, m³/h, L/s, L/min, US GPM, and CFM using standard defined factors, with cubic meters per second as the internal base unit since it is the SI-coherent unit for volumetric flow. It is worth being precise about which gallon is meant when a datasheet says "GPM" — this converter uses the US liquid gallon (a UK or imperial gallon is roughly 20% larger), which is the overwhelming convention for pump and irrigation equipment sold internationally, but it is always worth double-checking the source datasheet if the equipment has any UK or Commonwealth origin.

Typical use cases include converting a borewell or submersible pump's rated output from GPM (common on imported pump curves) into L/min or m³/h to match a local tank-filling time calculation, converting an HVAC supply fan's CFM rating into m³/h or L/s to cross-check against a room's required air changes per hour, sizing a cooling tower or process water pump where the vendor curve is in US units but the plant's piping and instrumentation are specified in metric, and converting a firefighting or drainage pump's flow rate between L/s and m³/h for fire code compliance documentation, which is typically written in metric terms in India.

Because flow rate figures directly drive pipe sizing, pump head calculations, and motor power sizing for the driving pump, a conversion error here can cascade into an undersized or oversized pump selection. The explicit conversion factor shown alongside every result, together with the quick-reference table showing the same flow expressed in all six units at once, is intended to make it easy to catch an order-of-magnitude mistake — for example, confusing L/min with L/s, which is a factor-of-60 error that is easy to make when reading a datasheet quickly.

Flow rate is also central to water treatment and effluent plant sizing, where design capacity is usually documented in m³/h or m³/day for regulatory filings, but individual dosing pumps, chemical metering equipment, and imported membrane or filtration skids may specify flow in L/min or GPM. Converting all of these into one consistent unit before summing a treatment train's stage-by-stage capacity avoids the compounding error that comes from adding flow figures across mismatched units, which would otherwise silently produce a plant capacity estimate that is off by whatever ratio separates the units involved.

📶 Frequency Converter — Hz, kHz, MHz, GHz, RPM

Base unit: Hertz (Hz)

Frequency conversion in an electrical and mechanical engineering context most often means relating rotational speed (RPM) to electrical frequency (Hz), which is one of the most fundamental relationships in motor and generator work. An induction motor's synchronous speed is directly determined by the supply frequency and the number of poles in the winding, following the relationship RPM equals 120 times frequency in Hz divided by the number of poles — which is why this converter treats 1 Hz as equal to 60 RPM, the direct one-to-one relationship used when RPM refers to revolutions per second scaled to per-minute, independent of any specific motor's pole count.

Beyond the electromechanical use case, this converter also handles the standard SI frequency scale — kilohertz, megahertz, and gigahertz — which is relevant for instrumentation, communications equipment, control system clock speeds, and any datasheet quoting a sampling rate, signal frequency, or bandwidth figure. Because these are simple powers-of-1,000 multiples of the hertz, converting between kHz, MHz, and GHz is straightforward, but having them alongside the RPM relationship in one tool saves switching between a general unit converter and a motor-specific calculator.

Common real-world scenarios include converting a variable frequency drive (VFD) output frequency into an expected motor RPM to verify a pump or fan will run within its designed speed range, checking whether a generator's rated frequency (50 Hz standard in India, versus 60 Hz common in North America) will drive a connected motor at the correct speed if the motor was originally rated for the other frequency standard, converting a mechanical tachometer reading in RPM back into an equivalent electrical frequency for troubleshooting a VFD or soft-starter fault code that references frequency rather than speed, and converting communications or control system frequency specifications between Hz, kHz, and MHz when comparing datasheets from different manufacturers that don't use consistent unit prefixes.

It's worth noting that the RPM-to-Hz relationship used here (1 Hz = 60 RPM) is the direct rotational-to-electrical mapping and does not itself account for a specific motor's pole count or any slip in an induction motor's actual running speed versus its synchronous speed — for a full motor speed calculation accounting for poles and slip, this converter is best used as a quick frequency-to-rotational-speed reference alongside, rather than a replacement for, a dedicated motor speed formula.

The 50 Hz versus 60 Hz question also matters whenever equipment is relocated internationally or a plant sources a motor, transformer, or generator from a 60 Hz market for use on India's 50 Hz grid. Running a 60 Hz-rated motor on a 50 Hz supply reduces its synchronous speed proportionally (by roughly one-sixth) and can affect cooling fan performance and torque characteristics, so converting the rated frequency and understanding the resulting speed shift is an important first check before commissioning any frequency-mismatched imported equipment, well before the electrical rating itself is even considered. Transformers behave differently again — running a transformer designed for 60 Hz on a 50 Hz supply increases its core flux density proportionally, which can push it toward magnetic saturation and elevated no-load losses, so frequency conversion here is a safety and thermal consideration, not just a speed calculation.

🚀 Speed Converter — m/s, km/h, mph, knot, ft/s

Base unit: Meter/second (m/s)

Linear speed conversion covers the everyday engineering need to move between meters per second (the SI-coherent unit used in most mechanical calculations, such as conveyor belt speed, fluid velocity, and fan tip speed), kilometers per hour (the everyday unit for vehicle speed and wind speed reporting in India), miles per hour (common on imported vehicle, wind turbine, and weather instrumentation datasheets), knots (the standard unit for marine and aviation speed, including wind speed on some meteorological and offshore engineering datasheets), and feet per second (frequently used in fluid velocity calculations and some US-origin fan or duct design standards).

The conversion constants used are the internationally defined values: 1 km/h equals 0.2777778 m/s (derived directly from 1,000 meters divided by 3,600 seconds), 1 mph equals 0.44704 m/s exactly (since a mile and an hour are both exactly defined), 1 knot equals 0.5144444 m/s (defined as exactly 1 nautical mile per hour, where a nautical mile is 1,852 meters), and 1 ft/s equals 0.3048 m/s, following directly from the foot's exact definition.

Typical applications include converting a conveyor belt's design speed from m/s into a more intuitive m/min or comparing it against a vendor's ft/s rating on an imported conveyor motor gearbox, checking a wind turbine's cut-in and rated wind speed (often quoted in mph or m/s depending on the manufacturer's home market) against a local meteorological wind speed dataset reported in km/h, converting a fluid or air velocity from a US engineering standard in ft/s into m/s for a metric process design calculation, and converting a marine vessel or offshore platform's design wind or current speed from knots into m/s for a structural load calculation.

Because speed conversion, like length and weight, is a pure multiplicative relationship with no offset, the quick-reference table lets you see one entered speed value expressed across all five units simultaneously, which is a fast way to build intuition for the relative scale of each unit — for instance, recognizing that a wind speed of "10" means something very different depending on whether it's in m/s, km/h, or knots is exactly the kind of unit-confusion error this tool is designed to prevent before it affects a design margin or safety calculation.

Speed conversion also matters for rotating equipment safety: a fan or grinding wheel's maximum permissible tip speed is a critical safety limit usually documented in m/s, but derived from a rotational RPM figure and a wheel or fan diameter. When cross-checking a fan's actual operating tip speed against an imported bearing or fan datasheet quoted in ft/s, converting correctly before comparing against the rated limit is essential, since operating even modestly above a rated tip speed can be a genuine mechanical safety hazard rather than just a performance shortfall. Vehicle and generator-set governor speed settings are another common case — a genset's engine speed is normally referenced in RPM, but its governor droop and frequency stability are sometimes documented in relation to a linear belt or fan speed in ft/s on an imported skid package, making a quick, accurate speed conversion useful during commissioning checks.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is temperature conversion different from the other categories? +

Length, weight, pressure, and similar units all scale from zero — doubling a length in meters doubles it in feet too. Temperature scales don't share a common zero point (0°C is not 0°F), so converting between Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin requires an offset-based formula rather than a simple multiplication factor.

How accurate are these conversions? +

All conversion factors used here are standard, widely-published SI and imperial conversion constants (e.g. 1 inch = 25.4 mm exactly, 1 bar = 100,000 Pa exactly). Results are computed to full floating-point precision and displayed rounded for readability — for extremely tight tolerance engineering work, always cross-check against a certified reference table.

Can I convert RPM using the Frequency converter? +

Yes — RPM is included as a unit inside the Frequency converter, since rotational speed and frequency are directly related (1 Hz = 60 RPM). This is useful when comparing a motor's rated RPM against a frequency-based spec like a VFD output frequency.

Does the Flow Rate converter handle both volume and mass flow? +

This converter covers volumetric flow rate units only (m³/s, m³/h, L/s, L/min, US GPM, and CFM). Mass flow rate (kg/s, lb/h, etc.) depends on fluid density and isn't a fixed unit conversion, so it isn't included here.
