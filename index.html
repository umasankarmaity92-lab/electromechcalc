05 — FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is kVA, and how is it different from kW? +

kVA (kilovolt-amperes) is apparent power — the total power flowing in an AC circuit, including both the power doing useful work and the power lost to reactive loads like motor coils. kW (kilowatts) is real power, the portion that actually performs work. The two are related by power factor: kW = kVA × PF. A power factor of 1.0 means all apparent power is real power; most industrial loads run between 0.8 and 0.95.

Why is Power Factor important when sizing equipment? +

Power factor determines how much of the delivered apparent power (kVA) is actually converted into usable work (kW). A low power factor means transformers, generators, and cables must be rated larger to deliver the same real power, raising equipment cost and line losses. Many utilities also penalize industrial customers for poor power factor, which is why correction capacitors (APFC panels) are common on factory floors.

How do I choose the right transformer or DG size for my load? +

Add up your total connected load in kW, divide by the expected power factor to get kVA, then add a safety margin — typically 20–25% — for future load growth and motor starting surges. Our Transformer Size and DG Size calculators handle this automatically and round up to the nearest standard rating.

Are these calculators accurate enough for professional use? +

Yes — every formula used is the same standard formula taught in electrical and mechanical engineering coursework and referenced in field handbooks. That said, for safety-critical or code-compliance decisions, always verify results against your local electrical code and have them reviewed by a licensed engineer. The EPF, SIP, and CAGR calculators follow the standard methods used by EPFO and major mutual fund houses, and are for educational planning purposes only — they do not constitute financial advice.
